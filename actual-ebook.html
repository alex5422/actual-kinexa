The problem has a name

You don't have a time problem. You have an attention problem. And attention, unlike time, can be cultivated.

The human brain was not designed for the continuous information flow that defines modern leadership. Emails before dawn, real-time dashboards, permanent decision-making: these environments systematically exhaust the prefrontal cortex — the seat of judgment, creativity, and long-term vision.

The result is not burnout exactly. It is something subtler and more damaging: brilliant leaders who react instead of anticipate. Who execute instead of imagine. Who survive instead of decide.

AI amplifies all of this — but it also, used well, creates space. The leaders who will navigate the next decade are not those who process faster. They are those who think better. These are different skills, trained differently.

"I spend 80% of my working day reading and thinking." — Warren Buffett, on his actual calendar

The historical record

Before mindfulness apps, before executive coaching — the leaders who shaped history had found their own protocols. Marcus Aurelius governed an empire and kept a private philosophical journal at the edge of military camps. Churchill painted during the Blitz. Lincoln memorised Shakespeare on horseback during the Civil War. Bill Gates still takes two weeks a year with no meetings and a stack of books.

None called it "wellness." They called it thinking.

The three antidotes

History. Not as nostalgia — as perspective. The leaders who understood the present moment best were those who could place it in a longer arc. The Romans governed better because they studied the Greeks. The best AI ethicists today are often historians.

Beauty. Neuroscience now confirms what artists knew: exposure to beauty activates the reward circuit, reduces cortisol, and restores sustained attention. Twenty minutes in front of a Rothko produces measurable cognitive restoration. This is not soft — it is physiology.

Reading. Not scanning. Reading long-form, with a pencil, without a destination. Difficult books train the working memory to hold complex narrative threads — directly improving the capacity to manage ambiguity. A leader who reads literary fiction makes better decisions under uncertainty. This is documented.

Eight books. Read slowly. In any order.

Stoicism
Meditations
Marcus Aurelius · 2nd century
A Roman emperor's private journal. Never meant to be published. The oldest known anti-cognitive-overload protocol.
Neuroscience
Thinking, Fast and Slow
Daniel Kahneman · 2011
The scientific map of why cognitive overload destroys decision quality. Nobel Prize. No shortcuts.
Attention
Stolen Focus
Johann Hari · 2022
The most rigorous investigation into the attention crisis. What Silicon Valley actually built — and knew it was building.
Deep work
Deep Work
Cal Newport · 2016
The capacity for sustained concentration is the rare skill of the 21st century. Newport explains how to recover it.
Brain & internet
The Shallows
Nicholas Carr · 2010
How digital tools literally rewire the brain — and why long-form reading remains the most powerful resistance.

Flow

The psychology of optimal experience. How to design a day that produces the state of complete absorption — not just survive it.

Why beauty makes us more just — and more capable of good judgment. Harvard philosopher. Short. Unsettling.
Long view

70,000 years of human history in 500 pages. The absolute antidote to the tyranny of the urgent.

The reading circle — six rules

01 — Who
4 to 8 people. Different roles.
No existing hierarchy. One criterion only: intellectual curiosity. Mixed profiles produce the most fertile cognitive friction.
02 — Where
Not a meeting room.
A library, a museum, a restaurant with character. The environment conditions the quality of thought. Beautiful spaces produce better thinking — this is not metaphor.
03 — How often
Once a month. No more.
Rare enough to be an event. Regular enough to create a thread. Duration: 2h30 maximum. The real conversations happen at dinner, after.
04 — Reading
With a pencil. Mark resistance.
Not what interests you — what disturbs you. Write one question per marked passage. Not a comment: a question. That is where thought begins.
05 — Format
Rotating facilitator.
Each member leads one session. Three open questions prepared in advance. One passage read aloud (5 minutes). No one dominates. Phones absent — non-negotiable.
06 — Close
One sentence each.
Each person says what they take from the session in a single sentence. Then vote on the next book. That's it. No summary document. No follow-up email.
A note on surnames & pen names
Actual publishes signed positions — because anonymous thought carries no weight and no accountability. But we recognise that a position on AI, medicine, or institutional governance can have professional consequences for its author. Actual therefore accepts contributions under a verified pen name: a pseudonym known to the editorial team, registered, traceable if legally required, but not public by default.
This is not new: Voltaire, George Orwell, George Sand, Stendhal — the tradition of the signed pseudonym is as old as the tradition of signing. The name protects the person. The position remains accountable. Both matter.
